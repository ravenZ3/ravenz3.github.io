title: "Thin Stick"
date: 2025-05-15
kind: prose
stage: budding
---

Yesterday was absurd. The kind of absurd that makes you wonder if life is scripted by someone with a bad sense of humor and an addiction to tragic irony.

I decided I deserved one day off. A holiday from the guilt, the pressure, the creeping suspicion that I’m messing everything up in slow motion. Even if I am, even if I’ve dismantled my own plans with surgical precision that takes effort too. It takes cranial destruction. Mental acrobatics. I’ve tried, faltered, tried again. So, rest. I earned it, even if barely.

Naturally, that’s the day my father chooses benevolence. “Let’s surprise your aunt,” he says. His sister. The one I’ve mentally nicknamed Nurse Ratched because even her smiles feel like medical procedures. “It’ll be a nice gesture,” he says. So we get a cake.

Surprises require secrecy. My grandmother, ever the covert agent, helps by calling my aunt to tell her... to sleep early. Sleep early. Surprise ruined at the speed of good intentions. Bless her heart.

We arrive. I walk into the house, and the air shifts. The greetings don’t come in words, but in shrapnel.

“You look terrible.”
“What happened to you?”
“So thin. No one will marry you. You’ll die alone.”
“Are you even eating?”
“Stick. He looks like a stick.”

They say these things casually, with the efficiency of people who have said them a hundred times before. Like customs officers at the emotional border, confiscating your peace.

No hello. No how have you been. Just an audit of my appearance, a public weighing of flesh and worth. I stand there, nodding, absorbing. They look at me as if I’ve failed some invisible test. Failed to exist in the correct proportion.

I don’t cry. I don’t retaliate. I don’t even speak. I just register. Like I always do. The day is ruined. The next day too, because shame is the kind of guest that lingers.

But I’m learning.

I’m learning that there are voices I don’t have to obey. That I can choose where to place my attention – on their noise, or on my own quiet. That survival, even silent, is a protest. That not responding is sometimes resistance.

Let them throw their arrows.
